Exploratory analysis of 6.9M CFPB cases spanning 13 years. Uncovers that credit-report errors drive 32% of all complaints, a post-2020 volume spike tied to the pandemic, and persistent geographic hotspots in FL, TX, and CA.

Why it matters

Business Problem

Rising CFPB complaint volumes create reputational, compliance, and revenue risks for banks and fintechs. Without a structured view of where and why complaints surge, firms struggle to prioritize fixes, allocate resources, and stay ahead of regulators.

Objective Business Value
Early-warning radar Detect product failures before they trigger fines or class-action suits.
Peer benchmarking Compare complaint rates by product and state to expose outliers.
Root-cause targeting Trace surges to specific workflows (e.g., credit-report fixes) and redesign them.
Resource allocation Focus CX budgets on hotspots (FL, TX, CA) and high-risk categories.
Regulatory readiness Give risk committees KPIs that pre-empt CFPB investigations.

How it was built

Data & Methodology

Analysis covers 6.9 million CFPB complaints from January 2011 to March 2024, downloaded via the public API and processed entirely in R.

1
Ingest & type-casting Load raw CSV and convert dates and factors with readr::read_csv. Handle encoding issues in narrative text fields.
2
Data cleaning Remove duplicates and records with empty narratives (0.6% of dataset) using dplyr filter chains.
3
Date enrichment Extract Year, Month, and Quarter fields via lubridate for time-series aggregation and seasonality checks.
4
Geo-join Map two-letter state codes to FIPS codes using usmap and sf for choropleth map generation.
5
Visual EDA Produce heat-maps, ranked bar charts, and time-series line plots with ggplot2. Export to Looker Studio for interactive drill-down.

Limitation: the dataset only captures consumers who filed formal complaints. Silent dissatisfaction is not recorded, so absolute volumes understate total friction.

What I found

Key Insights

01 32% of all complaints since 2011 are credit-report errors — the single largest category by a wide margin, ahead of debt collection (18%) and mortgage (11%). This concentration points to systemic friction in the credit bureau dispute process.
02 Complaint volume doubled post-2020 vs. the pre-COVID baseline. The 2021–2022 surge aligns with pandemic-era financial hardship, stimulus fraud, and a wave of credit-freeze requests from newly unemployed consumers.
03 FL, TX, and CA generate 27% of total complaint volume despite representing ~26% of the US population — a slight overrepresentation explained by their concentration of large financial institutions and high immigrant populations unfamiliar with dispute processes.
04 Timely response rates declined after 2020. The share of complaints receiving a response within 15 days dropped from 97% (2019) to 91% (2022), suggesting that complaint volume growth outpaced institutional response capacity.

Where this goes next

Recommendations & Next Steps

Monthly KPI dashboard for risk teams Publish a live credit-report complaint tracker with automated alerts when volume exceeds rolling-average thresholds.
NLP topic modelling on narratives Apply LDA or BERTopic to the free-text complaint narratives to surface emerging issue clusters before they become trends in the structured data.
Pilot chatbot resolution in hotspot states Target FL, TX, and CA with automated dispute-assistance tools to reduce formal complaint filings and improve CX scores.

Conclusions

The CFPB dataset reveals persistent systemic failures in credit reporting and debt collection. Geographic concentration in the three largest states is real but partially population-driven — the more actionable signal is the response-time degradation post-2020, which represents a direct operational risk for any institution under CFPB supervision.

The pronounced uptick post-2020 underscores how macroeconomic shocks translate directly into consumer-finance friction, and how quickly complaint volumes can outpace the capacity built for normal operating conditions.
